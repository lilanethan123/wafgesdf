A frontier era in heart medicine

Cardiac Surgery: The First Touches of the Heart

Between 1918 and 1940, surgeons challenged the belief that the heart was untouchable. Early procedures on the pericardium, wartime trauma care, and daring valve experiments set the stage for modern cardiac surgery.

Overview

Medicine at the Edge of the Possible

Cardiac surgery in this era was feared for bleeding, infection, and the belief that touching the heart meant certain death. Yet persistence and incremental advances laid the groundwork for later open-heart procedures.

Medical Beliefs

  • The heart was viewed as too vital and fragile to operate on.
  • Uncontrollable bleeding and infection were primary fears.
  • Most procedures stayed external or indirect.
  • Successful heart surgery was considered nearly impossible.

Professional Resistance

  • Many physicians believed heart operations violated medical ethics.
  • Medical journals discouraged experimentation, fearing public mistrust.
  • Some hospitals banned heart surgery entirely.

"A surgeon who attempts to operate on the heart should lose the respect of his colleagues."

Theodor Billroth

Scientific Knowledge

What Doctors Knew - and What They Couldn't Yet Do

Established Understanding

  • Basic cardiac anatomy, circulation, and heart function were known.
  • Physical examination and patient symptoms guided diagnosis.

Missing Capabilities

  • No reliable way to stop or bypass the heart.
  • No detailed imaging of internal cardiac structures.
  • Electrocardiography existed but was limited and not widely available.

Methods & Limitations

Why Outcomes Were Hard to Predict

Diagnostic Constraints

  • Clinicians relied on stethoscopes, exam findings, and history.
  • Early ECGs existed but were limited and inconsistently available.
  • No internal imaging meant valve and defect details were uncertain.

Unpredictable Outcomes

  • Hidden pathology made surgical risk hard to estimate.
  • Shock, blood loss, and infection could shift rapidly.
  • Surgeons often entered cases without definitive diagnoses.

Key Advances

How the Field Started Moving Forward

Pericardial Surgery

Surgeons treated pericardial effusions, infections, and constrictive pericarditis while avoiding the heart muscle itself.

Cardiac Injury Treatment

World War I trauma care taught surgeons how to suture heart wounds, control bleeding, and manage shock. Fine needles and silk sutures improved repair, and emergencies were sometimes done without anesthesia.

Early Valve Attempts

In 1925, Henry Souttar performed a mitral stenosis operation using a finger inserted into the heart, a rare but groundbreaking step. Mitral stenosis often followed rheumatic fever, restricting blood flow and causing fatigue and breathlessness.

Then vs Now

Closed-Heart to Open-Heart

Drag the handle to compare early closed-heart practice with modern open-heart techniques made possible by cardiopulmonary bypass.

Then (1918-1940)

  • Procedures were closed-heart or indirect.
  • Surgeons operated without a heart-lung machine.
  • Valve and congenital defects were often treated "blind."

Now

  • Open-heart surgery with direct vision is standard.
  • Bypass keeps blood circulating during repair.
  • Valve and defect repairs are routine.

Improvements

Technology and Medical Progress

What Changed

  • Safer and more reliable anesthesia.
  • Antiseptic and sterile techniques reduced infection.
  • More precise surgical instruments.
  • Better understanding of cardiac anatomy.

What Was Still Missing

  • No heart-lung machine.
  • No open-heart surgery.
  • Limited imaging and diagnostics.
  • Very high mortality rates.

Operating Reality

What Surgery Looked Like in Practice

Surgical Environment

  • Basic operating rooms with limited lighting.
  • Manual instruments and no electronic monitoring.
  • Surgeons relied on pulse, skin color, and breathing.

Anesthesia and Sterility

  • Ether allowed longer procedures; chloroform acted fast but risked death.
  • Local anesthesia was used for emergency cases.
  • Stricter hygiene and sterilized tools helped, but no antibiotics existed.

Risk and Ethics

High Mortality, Limited Consent

Mortality and Risk

  • Mortality often ranged from 30% to over 70%.
  • Procedures were sometimes abandoned mid-operation.
  • Many patients were terminal cases with no alternatives.

Ethical Controversies

  • Surgeons debated whether experimentation justified the danger.
  • True informed consent was hard to achieve in emergencies.
  • Failures were common but rarely published.

Training and Spread

How Knowledge Moved, Slowly

Education

  • No dedicated cardiac surgery specialty existed.
  • General surgeons learned by observation and journals.
  • Caution slowed progress but reduced reckless experimentation.

Global Contributions

  • Europe led early experimentation and trauma repair.
  • British and French military hospitals refined wartime technique.
  • American hospitals expanded research in the 1930s.

Key Terms

Quick Glossary

Pericardium

The sac surrounding the heart; early surgery focused here first.

Commissurotomy

Manual separation of fused valve leaflets to restore blood flow.

Stenosis

Narrowing of a valve or opening, restricting circulation.

Shock

Life-threatening circulatory collapse from trauma or blood loss.

Importance

Why This Era Matters

The period between 1918 and 1940 challenged the belief that the heart was untouchable. The techniques and courage developed in this era prepared the medical world for open-heart surgery and life-saving cardiac procedures that followed.

Key Figures

People Who Shaped Early Cardiac Surgery

Henry Souttar

Performed a pioneering mitral valve operation in 1925, proving internal cardiac work could be attempted, yet the procedure was not widely adopted due to criticism and fear.

Theodor Billroth

His famous warning captured the skepticism of the era toward heart operations.

WWI Military Surgeons

Developed techniques for suturing heart injuries and managing trauma under pressure.

Conclusion

From Forbidden to Foundational

Cardiac surgery from 1918 to 1940 was defined by courage, experimentation, and resistance. Surgeons worked with limited tools, high risk, and little institutional support, yet proved the heart could survive surgical manipulation. By 1940, cardiac surgery was no longer unthinkable - only unfinished.
